I've been working on a review about perfectionism for a while now. Can't seem to get it to a place I'm happy with. All I see when I look at it is a pot of chicken soup.

Then Double Eleven rolled around. The so-called "Annual E-Commerce Dog-Kicking Conference."

Last night, a friend I met in the summer before senior year of high school asked me what being drunk felt like. I thought about it and answered: "It's like going crazy with the courage you don't have, saying the things you don't dare to say, crying tears you don't want to show. Then it knocks you out like a dead pig and lets you sleep it off."

Before she could respond, I asked: "Didn't snag your Double Eleven deals?"

She sent me a string of smile emojis.

I stayed quiet this time, just waiting for her to talk. I actually knew. She'd been through a breakup. I could tell from her posts — she'd stopped using the girly stickers she used to love in our chats.

Her ex-boyfriend was my classmate. He was in Guangzhou. She was in Shanghai. Apart more than together. They barely made it a year.

Student relationships, friendships — they're built on time spent together. Doing everything together, sharing the same feelings. It's natural to fall in love that way. But once you're separated, you see each other's lives with new, closer companions. Everything turns into suspicion. It's only human.

"对方正在输入…" flashed for a long time. Then, as expected — a long flood of confession. Singles' Day amplifies everything. All the feelings.

Suddenly a call came in. On the other end, she was crying. Cursing her useless ex-boyfriend. "And alcohol tastes this awful." Regretting everything.

For a second, it all seemed kind of funny. I laughed and said: "You're an idiot, hahaha!" Then I realized my mistake and hung up with a click.

What followed was predictable — a barrage of four-second voice messages filled with swear words.

"Keep going. Don't stop."

She cursed for a while. The mood lightened. She started sending flirty stickers, talking about what she'd bought and what she planned to grab. I couldn't get a word in. Just listened. Maybe she didn't even know what this conversation was supposed to mean.

A few minutes later she was back to being the girl I knew.

I thought about it afterward. What she needed wasn't to get drunk. It was to vent. You could also tear up paper, draw circles, find an old friend to yell at. Why bother getting drunk and making a scene? Failing that — go to the supermarket and crush some instant noodles.

Seriously. If you're alone, who are you performing your loneliness for?

There's always someone who doesn't want to see you drinking alone in the dead of night.

I'm writing this for this friend. And for all of you.

Don't go drinking alone in the dead of night.

And if you really are melancholy — find a friend. Go eat some dumplings or hotpot in the cold. Have a couple of drinks and talk. Take a quiet walk in the cold wind.
