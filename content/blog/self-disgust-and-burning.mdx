Hatred is a strong emotion. Very occasionally, I hate other people, or society. But I don't think I've ever truly hated myself. Regret, yes. Temporary adjustments, yes.

But the people and things around us that are hated, despised — they gradually learn to reflect. To work hard in silence. To change.

I'm not at the age or level to lecture. So I'll just say what I think. Think about what I can reach.

Today I want to talk about something you can't sum up in a single word: self-disgust.

A while ago, I saw a screenshot of a college classmate's notes on Moments. He seemed like a really good person. Warm, positive in front of others. We weren't close, so I can't say for sure. That was just the feeling.

In his notes he wrote:

> "The dorm lost power tonight. Dark and quiet. Perfect for sleeping. But I'm used to not sleeping at this hour. Lately I've been gaming until 4 or 5 AM, then sleeping until noon. Thursday and Friday mornings without class are fine. The other days, I skip whatever I can skip. What I can't skip, I just go through the motions. In my state of mind, I can't absorb anything in class anyway. Honestly, I'm not even addicted to gaming. It's just a way to make time pass faster.
>
> When everyone else doesn't have enough time and is buried in things to do, I'm here thinking time moves too slowly. Pretty ridiculous, isn't it?
>
> I've been running from reality. From facing this version of myself. For over a year I've fully committed to being a bad student. Never took studying seriously. Never did an assignment properly. Never even organized a single notebook. Just chasing pleasure without any drive.
>
> I have three regrets in college. First, I didn't join the student council freshman year. Everyone else had things to do, meetings to attend. I just watched dramas. Others fought for opportunities from day one, busy out of their minds. I told myself I was a cold, aloof学霸. I pushed away friends who wanted to get close. I ignored every organization. And the result? My ranking that semester was near the bottom. Pretty funny when I think about it now.
>
> Second regret: I didn't become a freshman mentor sophomore year. I didn't even try. My excuse was that I needed to study hard. (Same excuse as freshman year. Ha.)
>
> Third regret: I met a girl I liked. But I wasn't good enough for her. I had nothing attractive about me. No real chance to get to know her. I'd never even had a proper conversation with her alone. She felt several levels above me. My confession was a mess. I just caused her trouble.
>
> Of course, I've had moments of feverish resolve to catch up academically. But that feeling of powerlessness and confusion made me give up every time. I can't follow the listening exercises. I can't write. I barely know any vocabulary. I can't write anything without my phone's dictionary. Sometimes when the teacher tells me what to do, I go 'Huh?' a beat too slow. The worse I do, the less confidence I have to keep going. The further I fall behind, the more comfortable I become with being a bad student.
>
> What college student? What English major? Just labels to me. I'm embarrassed to say I study English. Because I don't deserve to.
>
> Usually at the end of a passage like this, you're supposed to set a resolution. But I really can't catch up anymore. College — I didn't live it well."

After reading this, I remember not liking the post. Not because I thought it was unworthy or beneath me. I just didn't know how to react. I didn't want to feed him chicken soup. Give him the "strength" he probably didn't need.

I thought about it for a long time afterward. And oddly enough, I felt kind of happy for him.

Look at history — East and West, economics, culture, politics, technology. On a macro level, things keep moving forward. But individual growth is always limited. Including the geniuses, the greats, the monsters. And often the limited but remarkable contributions they make in their lives come not from smooth sailing, but from twists and turns, transformation, or tragedy. Pain, dissatisfaction, desire — these push people to do what others won't.

I'm rambling again. Sorry. But I wanted to say it.

Back to this friend. We're both at this confused age. Like America's Lost Generation in *The Catcher in the Rye*. I too often feel troubled, disgusted with my own life. Sometimes you really need a push. Or someone to meet.

If these rambling words don't move you, then try talking to that girl again. Or set a resolution you won't post on Moments. Do it for yourself. Selfishly. Unconditionally. For once.

If everyone in life had to be understood and flattered, it would be exhausting. But there's one person I think you should take the time to truly understand, and treat well: yourself.

From the moment you start to feel disgusted with yourself — that's when girls and boys learn to hold up their own little sky. It brings low moods, negative attitudes, deepens how others' opinions affect you. It makes you struggle in your little world. Cry tears no one sees. Stare into space in ways no one notices. Then regret. Then change.

And all of that — it could be beautiful. The sayings about rainbows after storms are clichéd, appearing in every student essay, but they exist for a reason.

Though I have to say — if you feel satisfied with everything, isn't that a little sad too? Like someone on an island of self-intoxication. Sea fog hiding the lighthouse on the shipping route. The rushing world passing by. A gust of wind and they think they're the center of the vortex.

Maybe everyone defines happiness and fulfillment differently.

It's already cold. It's already dark. The power didn't go out tonight, but it's still dark and still quiet. Perfect for sleeping.

Maybe you're wandering in the neon-lit corner of some city. I can't say much else. Just that tonight, we are young. Here's to you — who dared to be disgusted with yourself today.
