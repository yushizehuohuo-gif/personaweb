Another turn of the year. Another sigh over the vows made at the start. Then I look back at a year that is far from perfect, but still quite brilliant.

The city around Christmas and New Year feels especially cluttered — the kind of clutter where everything from gold to shit gets wrapped up. Everything blushes coyly, waiting. Whether you're a barefoot peasant or Jesus Christ himself, these are the days to eat and drink and live it up.

Lu Xun once said: there were no holidays in the world to begin with, but when enough people celebrate them, they become holidays. There were two trees in front of his house. One was a Christmas tree. The other was also a Christmas tree.

Once upon a time, our parents' generation — or the generations before them — lived through an era of scarcity. When the season came around, they were still children then, wearing new floral jackets, eyes fixed on the hot dishes on the table. They would never forget those days. And so they passed them down. Generation after generation, until no one remembers anymore what that childhood sweetness felt like.

I'm rambling. If I keep going, I'll end up reminiscing about bitter days to appreciate the sweet ones and building a socialist worldview.

About gatherings. I can't quite say what a gathering is ultimately for. So I'll pick it apart bit by bit.

"When shall we get together?" we usually ask. And the person asked starts thinking: who will be there? How will this benefit my social circle, career, or personal growth? The guest list determines the social tier, the degree of integration, even the cost. Or maybe you're afraid of running into an ex. Maybe the memory of certain people still stings. Maybe you did something you're not proud of. Maybe there's someone you desperately want to see. Maybe this gathering is an opportunity. Maybe you've been alone too long and need to let loose.

In short: is it worth it?

Then comes the answer: "Sure!" or "Oh, I'd love to, but I have something that day."

Setting aside the ones that never happen, a typical gathering unfolds along the main storyline of eating and drinking. Polite chit-chat at the table. Among closer friends, teasing and laughter. Compliments and mutual praise — I don't mean this in a bad way, I'm just describing it. This is how emotional bonds get reinforced.

But the rise of social media has added extra layers to all of this, like the lingering notes of a perfume.

People pick special days to gather, and social networks tell everyone around you — at every tier — who you spent those important moments with. It reflects the quality of your life, the taste of your circle, all kinds of subtle signals for whoever's watching. But none of this comes with companionship. It often leaves you feeling hollow.

Eating alone. Drinking alone. Having fun alone. What others see is you, alone. It's as if doing these things in a crowd makes them psychologically more comfortable — like New Year during those scarce times, when a full meal was a rare luxury.

But today, we just pick a day to eat until we're bloated, drink until we're wrecked, and party until we're insane. Material abundance is no longer a problem. We don't have to save up all year for one good meal.

Painting these acts in such an unflattering light — should we still go to gatherings?

If you live the same twenty-four hours, three hundred and sixty-five days, year after year, all you can say is that you've done your part to keep the world running. And they don't give a shit.

Or perhaps you live a brilliant life, alone, always alone. No one to share your joy with, no one even knows about it. If you still feel happy that way, then all I can say is: friend, as long as you're happy.

Gathering — that's mostly what it is. Neither good nor bad. It stands like a blank tablet in the world.

But at the very end, I have to pour a bowl of chicken soup for the soul: in those moments after someone says "Oh, I'd love to, but I have something that day" — or after you say it to someone else — live well. Alone. In moderation. Positively. Brilliantly.

Catch up another time. Good night.
